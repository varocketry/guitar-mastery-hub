## üìñ SECTION 1: THE CLASSIC MINOR PROGRESSION (i-iv-V-i)

### The Minor Key I-IV-V

**In major keys:**  
**I-IV-V-I** = The universal progression

**In minor keys:**  
**i-iv-V-i** = The minor equivalent

**This is THE fundamental minor progression.**

---

### Building i-iv-V-i in A Minor

**A Minor diatonic chords:**
- i = Am (A-C-E)
- iv = Dm (D-F-A)
- V = E major (E-G#-B) ‚Üê from harmonic minor!
- i = Am (A-C-E)

**Progression: Am - Dm - E - Am**

**Why it works:**
```
Am (i)  = Tonic, home, stable (but minor = dark)
Dm (iv) = Subdominant, moves away from home
E (V)   = Major dominant (from harmonic minor), strong tension
Am (i)  = Tonic, resolution back to home
```

**This creates:**
- Departure from home (Am ‚Üí Dm)
- Tension (E major chord)
- Strong resolution (E ‚Üí Am)
- **Complete, resolved minor key feeling**

---

### Playing i-iv-V-i on Guitar

**A Minor: Am - Dm - E - Am**

```
Am:           Dm:           E:            Am:
E|---0---|    E|---1---|    E|---0---|    E|---0---|
B|---1---|    B|---3---|    B|---0---|    B|---1---|
G|---2---|    G|---2---|    G|---1---|    G|---2---|
D|---2---|    D|---0---|    D|---2---|    D|---2---|
A|---0---|    A|-------|    A|---2---|    A|---0---|
E|-------|    E|-------|    E|---0---|    E|-------|
```

**Play each chord for 4 beats at 60 BPM.**

**Listen for:**
- The dark, sad quality (minor tonic)
- The tension created by E major
- The satisfying resolution back to Am
- **The complete, circular feeling**

---

## üìñ SECTION 2: THE ANDALUSIAN CADENCE (i-VI-III-VII)

### The Spanish/Flamenco Progression

**The Andalusian cadence:**  
**i - VI - III - VII**

**This creates a descending bass line and a distinctly Spanish/dramatic sound.**

---

### Building the Andalusian Cadence in A Minor

**A Minor: Am - F - C - G**

**Analysis:**
- Am (i) = Tonic minor
- F (VI) = Submediant major (from natural minor)
- C (III) = Mediant major, relative major key
- G (VII) = Subtonic major (from natural minor)

**Bass line: A ‚Üí F ‚Üí C ‚Üí G** (descending in 3rds)

**This creates a descending, circular motion.**

---

### Playing the Andalusian Cadence

**Am - F - C - G (repeat)**

```
Am:           F:            C:            G:
E|---0---|    E|---1---|    E|---0---|    E|---3---|
B|---1---|    B|---1---|    B|---1---|    B|---0---|
G|---2---|    G|---2---|    G|---0---|    G|---0---|
D|---2---|    D|---3---|    D|---2---|    D|---0---|
A|---0---|    A|---3---|    A|---3---|    A|---2---|
E|-------|    E|---1---|    E|-------|    E|---3---|
```

**Play slowly, emphasizing the bass line.**

**Listen for:**
- Descending motion (A-F-C-G)
- Spanish/flamenco character
- Dramatic, passionate quality
- Circular feeling (loops back to Am)

---

### Emotional Character

**The Andalusian cadence sounds:**
- Passionate, dramatic
- Spanish, exotic
- Circular, hypnotic
- Intense, emotional

**Used in:**
- Flamenco guitar music
- Spanish-influenced rock/pop
- Dramatic film scores
- Songs needing intensity

---

### Famous Songs Using Andalusian Cadence

**"Sultans of Swing" - Dire Straits**
- Uses Andalusian progression
- Creates distinctive sound
- Rock with Spanish flavor

**"Runaway" - Del Shannon**
- Variation on Andalusian pattern
- '60s classic pop sound

**"Hit the Road Jack" - Ray Charles**
- Am - G - F - E progression (variant)
- Descending motion similar to Andalusian

**Flamenco Music:**
- Standard progression in flamenco
- Defines the genre's sound
- Often played with rasgueado technique

## üìñ SECTION 3: ROCK/METAL MINOR PROGRESSIONS

### The i-VII-VI-VII Pattern

**Rock and metal love this progression:**  
**i - VII - VI - VII**

**In A Minor: Am - G - F - G**

**Why it works in rock:**
- Uses only natural minor chords
- No leading tone needed (V not required)
- Power chord-friendly
- Strong, driving sound

---

### Playing i-VII-VI-VII

**Am - G - F - G (repeat)**

```
Am:           G:            F:            G:
E|---0---|    E|---3---|    E|---1---|    E|---3---|
B|---1---|    B|---0---|    B|---1---|    B|---0---|
G|---2---|    G|---0---|    G|---2---|    G|---0---|
D|---2---|    D|---0---|    D|---3---|    D|---0---|
A|---0---|    A|---2---|    A|---3---|    A|---2---|
E|-------|    E|---3---|    E|---1---|    E|---3---|
```

**Or with power chords (rock style):**

```
Am5:          G5:           F5:           G5:
E|-------|    E|-------|    E|-------|    E|-------|
B|-------|    B|-------|    B|-------|    B|-------|
G|-------|    G|-------|    G|-------|    G|-------|
D|---7---|    D|---5---|    D|---3---|    D|---5---|
A|---7---|    A|---5---|    A|---3---|    A|---5---|
E|---5---|    E|---3---|    E|---1---|    E|---3---|
```

**Strumming:** Heavy, driving rhythm

---

### Famous Songs Using i-VII-VI-VII

**"Stairway to Heaven" - Led Zeppelin (A minor section)**
- Uses Am-G-F-G type movement
- Creates mysterious, folk-rock quality
- One of the most famous progressions ever

**Metal/Rock Ballads:**
- Countless metal songs use this
- Driving but melancholic
- Perfect for minor key rock

---

### The i-VI-VII Pattern (Simplified)

**Even simpler: i - VI - VII**

**In A Minor: Am - F - G**

**Three chords, maximum impact:**
- Am (i) = Dark tonic
- F (VI) = Brightens slightly
- G (VII) = Drives back to Am

**Used in:**
- Rock anthems
- Folk-rock
- Simplified versions of longer progressions

## üìñ SECTION 4: MODAL MINOR PROGRESSIONS

### Using Natural Minor v (Not V Major)

**Some progressions use v (minor) instead of V (major):**

**i - v - VI - III**

**In A Minor: Am - Em - F - C**

**Character:**
- Softer than i-iv-V-i
- More modal/folk-like
- Less resolved (no leading tone)
- Floating, uncertain quality

---

### Playing i-v-VI-III

**Am - Em - F - C**

```
Am:           Em:           F:            C:
E|---0---|    E|---0---|    E|---1---|    E|---0---|
B|---1---|    B|---0---|    B|---1---|    B|---1---|
G|---2---|    G|---0---|    G|---2---|    G|---0---|
D|---2---|    D|---2---|    D|---3---|    D|---2---|
A|---0---|    A|---2---|    A|---3---|    A|---3---|
E|-------|    E|---0---|    E|---1---|    E|-------|
```

**Listen for:**
- Softer resolution (Em doesn't pull to Am as strongly as E major)
- Folk-like, modal quality
- Less dramatic than classical minor

---

### When to Use Modal Minor

**Use v (minor) instead of V (major) when:**
- You want softer, less dramatic resolution
- You're writing folk or indie music
- You want a floating, uncertain feeling
- You want to avoid classical minor sound

**Use V (major) when:**
- You want strong, definitive resolution
- You're writing classical-influenced music
- You want drama and intensity
- You want traditional harmonic function

## üìñ SECTION 5: MINOR BLUES PROGRESSIONS

### 12-Bar Minor Blues

**The blues scale works in both major and minor, but minor blues has its own progression:**

**Structure (12 bars):**
```
| i  | i  | i  | i  |
| iv | iv | i  | i  |
| V  | iv | i  | V  |
```

**In A Minor:**
```
| Am | Am | Am | Am |
| Dm | Dm | Am | Am |
| E  | Dm | Am | E  |
```

**Character:**
- Dark, bluesy, soulful
- Less bright than major blues
- Used in blues, soul, R&B

---

### Simplified Minor Blues (8-Bar)

**Shorter version:**
```
| i  | iv | i  | V  |
| i  | iv | i  | V  |
```

**In A Minor:**
```
| Am | Dm | Am | E  |
| Am | Dm | Am | E  |
```

**Great for:**
- Song sections
- Verses or choruses
- Simpler blues-influenced songs

## üìñ SECTION 6: CHOOSING PROGRESSIONS FOR EMOTION

### Emotional Qualities of Minor Progressions

**i-iv-V-i (Classic Minor):**
- **Emotion:** Serious, resolved sadness
- **Character:** Traditional, complete
- **Use for:** Classical-influenced, traditional sad songs
- **Examples:** Classical music, ballads

---

**i-VI-III-VII (Andalusian):**
- **Emotion:** Passionate, dramatic
- **Character:** Spanish, intense
- **Use for:** Drama, passion, intensity
- **Examples:** Flamenco-influenced rock, dramatic moments

---

**i-VII-VI-VII (Rock Minor):**
- **Emotion:** Dark, driving
- **Character:** Powerful, anthemic
- **Use for:** Rock ballads, metal, driving songs
- **Examples:** "Stairway to Heaven," metal ballads

---

**i-v-VI-III (Modal Minor):**
- **Emotion:** Soft sadness, uncertainty
- **Character:** Folk-like, floating
- **Use for:** Indie, folk, introspective songs
- **Examples:** Folk music, indie rock

---

**i-iv-i-V (Blues Minor):**
- **Emotion:** Soulful, bluesy
- **Character:** Groovy, rhythmic
- **Use for:** Blues, soul, R&B
- **Examples:** Blues standards, soul music

---

### Choosing Based on Intent

**Ask yourself:**
1. **What emotion am I trying to convey?**
   - Resolved sadness ‚Üí i-iv-V-i
   - Drama/passion ‚Üí i-VI-III-VII
   - Dark power ‚Üí i-VII-VI-VII
   - Soft melancholy ‚Üí i-v-VI-III

2. **What genre am I writing?**
   - Classical/traditional ‚Üí i-iv-V-i
   - Rock/metal ‚Üí i-VII-VI-VII
   - Folk/indie ‚Üí i-v-VI-III
   - Blues/soul ‚Üí Minor blues

3. **Do I need strong resolution?**
   - Yes ‚Üí Use V (major)
   - No ‚Üí Use v (minor) or VII
