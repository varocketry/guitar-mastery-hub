## üéØ CONNECTING THE DOTS

**Right now, when you change chords, you jump.**

**One chord... switch... next chord.**

**Clean. Direct. Functional.**

**But listen to any country, folk, or blues recording.**

**The chords don't just JUMP. They WALK.**

**Between C and G, there's movement:**
```
Not this:
C [strum-strum-strum] ‚Üí [jump] ‚Üí G [strum-strum-strum]
Static switch, abrupt change

But this:
C [strum-strum-strum] ‚Üí [bass notes: C-D-E-F#] ‚Üí G [arrives!]
Musical journey, smooth transition
```

**Those connecting bass notes are called WALKING BASS LINES.**

**And they're what make acoustic guitar sound like a BAND instead of just a rhythm instrument.**

---

### What Walking Bass Lines Actually Do

**Walking bass lines create:**

1. **Smooth transitions** (chords connect musically, not abruptly)
2. **Forward momentum** (music keeps moving, not static)
3. **Professional sound** (like studio recordings, not bedroom practice)
4. **Rhythm interest** (movement between chords, not just on chords)

**The difference:**

**Amateur:** C ‚Üí G (jump, static)  
**Professional:** C ‚Üí walk-up (C-D-E-F#) ‚Üí G (connected, smooth)

**Same chords. Different impact.**

**Walking bass = the difference between "plays guitar" and "sounds like a record."**

---

### Where This Comes From

**Country music tradition:**

**In the 1950s-70s, country bands had:**
- Acoustic guitar (chords)
- Bass player (root notes + walking lines)
- Together = full, connected sound

**Solo acoustic guitarists wanted that SAME sound:**
- Play chords (guitar role)
- Add walking bass lines (bass role)
- **One guitarist = sounds like two instruments**

**This technique created:**
- Merle Haggard's sound
- Johnny Cash's acoustic style
- Traditional country/folk recordings
- Modern country acoustic guitar

**When you hear a country acoustic guitarist sound "full," they're playing walking bass lines.**

### The Two Types You're Learning

**Type 1: Walk-Ups** (Going UP to the next chord)
```
From C to G:
C chord ‚Üí bass notes go UP: C - D - E - F# - arrive at G
            (each note climbing higher)

Creates: Anticipation, building energy
Used in: Country, folk, bluegrass
Example: Intro to "Folsom Prison Blues"
```

**Type 2: Walk-Downs** (Going DOWN from a chord)
```
From G to D:
G chord ‚Üí bass notes go DOWN: G - F# - E - D
            (each note descending)

Creates: Resolution, settling down
Used in: Blues, country, folk endings
Example: Classic country turnarounds
```

**These aren't random notes.**

**They're CONNECTING tones between chord roots.**

**They create musical LOGIC and DIRECTION.**

**Psychological impact:**

**Jumping between chords:**
- Brain hears: disconnected events

**Walking between chords:**

**Music tells a story:**

**This is why country and folk music feels like STORYTELLING.**

**The bass lines literally tell musical stories.**

**Walking bass lines require PERFECT timing.**

**Why?**

Each bass note must land EXACTLY on a beat:
```
Beat: 1   2   3   4   1
Note: C   D   E   F#  G
      ‚Üì   ‚Üì   ‚Üì   ‚Üì   ‚Üì

- Rhythm falls apart
- Pattern sounds sloppy
- Professional sound lost
```

**This is why starting tempo is 20 BPM.**

**At 20 BPM, you can:**
- Place finger on correct fret
- Pluck on exact beat
- Move to next position
- Maintain timing throughout

**Not speed training. Not technique training.**

**TIMING training.**

## üìñ THEORY: What Are Walking Bass Lines?

### Understanding Walking Bass Lines

**Walking bass line** = Single notes played on bass strings that "walk" stepwise from one chord to another, creating smooth transitions.

**Think of it like stairs:**
- Jumping from one chord to another = taking a big leap
- Walking bass line = taking small steps between shapes
- Each note is a "step" toward the next chord
- Smooth, musical transition instead of abrupt change

**Where you hear walking bass:**
- Country music (Johnny Cash, Willie Nelson, Merle Haggard)
- Folk music (most acoustic songs)
- Blues (Robert Johnson, Muddy Waters)
- Rockabilly (Elvis, Carl Perkins)
- Singer-songwriter (James Taylor, John Denver)

**Professional sound:**
Walking bass lines are what separate beginner strummers from professional-sounding players!

---

### Two Types of Walking Bass Lines

**Type 1: Country-Style Walks**
- Use notes from the major scale only
- Stepwise motion (C‚ÜíD‚ÜíE‚ÜíF or F‚ÜíE‚ÜíD‚ÜíC)
- Clean, traditional sound
- **This is what we'll learn first!**

**Type 2: Blues-Style Walks**
- Add chromatic passing tones (notes outside the scale)
- More sophisticated sound
- Common in blues, jazz, rockabilly
- **We'll learn this second!**

**Both types use the same basic concept - we're just changing which notes we use.**

---

### The Musical Function

**Walking bass lines serve multiple purposes:**

**1. Smooth transitions**
- Connect chords musically (not just jumping between them)
- Fill up time during shape changes
- Create forward momentum

**2. Bass line movement**
- Add interest to repetitive progressions
- Create foundation for melody
- Give bass guitar players something to follow (if you play with a band)

**3. Professional sound**
- Makes simple songs sound more sophisticated
- Shows musical knowledge
- Impresses listeners (they may not know WHY it sounds good, but they hear it!)

---

### Common Chord Progressions Using Walks

**In the key of C, we focus on three chords:**
- **C** (I chord - "home")
- **F** (IV chord - "subdominant")
- **G** (V chord - "dominant")

**These three chords appear in thousands of songs!**

**Classic progressions:**
- C ‚Üí F ‚Üí C ‚Üí G (most common)
- C ‚Üí F ‚Üí G ‚Üí C (also very common)
- C ‚Üí Am ‚Üí F ‚Üí G (pop progression)

**Walking bass lines work in ALL of these!**

## üé∏ COUNTRY-STYLE WALKING BASS LINES

### Walk #1: C to F (Walk-Up)

**The movement:** We're going UP from C to F

**The bass line:**
```
TAB:
e|------------------
B|------------------
G|------------------
D|--------0--1--2---  ‚Üê Land on F chord
A|--3--3------------  ‚Üê Start on C chord
E|------------------

Finger: p   p   p  p  p
Count:  1   2   3  4  (1 = start F chord)
```

**Step-by-step:**
1. **Beat 1:** A string, 3rd fret (root of C chord - already pressing it!)
2. **Beat 2:** A string, 3rd fret (same note again)
3. **Beat 3:** D string, open (0 - lift fingers)
4. **Beat 4:** D string, 1st fret (index finger)
5. **Next beat 1:** D string, 2nd fret - **you've arrived at F chord!**

**Left hand positioning:**
- Start: C chord position
- Beat 3: Release C chord, play open D
- Beat 4: Index on 1st fret (but keep pinky/ring ready for F)
- Next beat: Form full F chord

---

### Walk #1 Practice Routine

**Stage 1: Bass Notes Only (Days 1-2, 20 BPM)**

**Set metronome to 20 BPM**

**Just play the walk, no chords:**
```
A string: 3  3  
D string: 0  1  2

Count:    1  2  3  4  (land on F)
```

**Instructions:**
1. Form C-shape with left hand
2. Play A string (3rd fret) twice
3. Play D string open
4. Play D string 1st fret
5. Form F chord (you're there!)
6. Repeat 20 times

**Goal:** Every note clear, lands on correct beat

---

**Stage 2: Add Simple Strumming (Days 3-4, 30 BPM)**

**Increase to 30 BPM**

**Two measures total:**
```
Measure 1: C chord with Root-Down-Up pattern
           Count: 1  2  &  3  4  &

Measure 2: Walk from C to F
           Count: 1  2  3  4  (arrive at F)
```

**Instructions:**
1. Play C chord for one measure (4 beats) using Root-Down-Up pattern
2. On measure 2, play the walk (bass notes only)
3. Land on F chord
4. Repeat

**Practice 15 minutes daily for 2 days**

## üé∏ BLUES-STYLE WALKING BASS LINES

### What Makes Blues Walks Different?

**Country walks:** Use only scale notes (diatonic)  
**Blues walks:** Add chromatic passing tones (notes between scale notes)

**Example:**
- Country walk from C to F: C ‚Üí D ‚Üí E ‚Üí F (skips notes)
- Blues walk from C to F: C ‚Üí C# ‚Üí D ‚Üí D# ‚Üí E ‚Üí F (includes ALL half-steps!)

**The sound:**
- Country: Clean, traditional, predictable
- Blues: Sophisticated, jazzy, professional

**Same chords, different notes!**

---

### Blues Walk #1: C to F (Walk-Up with Chromatic Notes)

**The bass line:**
```
TAB:
e|------------------
B|------------------
G|------------------
D|--------0--1--2---  ‚Üê Land on F chord (3rd fret)
A|--3---------------  ‚Üê Start on C chord
E|------------------

Count:  1   2   3  4  (1 = arrive at F)
```

**Wait, this looks the same!** 

**Yes! But listen to the SOUND - we're adding chromatic color:**

**Enhanced version:**
```
TAB:
e|------------------
B|------------------
G|------------------
D|--------0--1--2---
A|--3---------------
E|------------------

Finger: Ring  Index  Middle  Ring
Count:  1     2      3       4
```

**The notes:** C (A string, 3rd) ‚Üí D (D string, 0) ‚Üí Eb (D string, 1st) ‚Üí E (D string, 2nd) ‚Üí **F** (D string, 3rd)

**Key difference:** We're thinking of these as chromatic steps, not just "any notes that get us there."

---

### Blues Walk #2: F to C (Walk-Down with Slide)

**The bass line:**
```
TAB:
e|------------------
B|------------------
G|------------------
D|--3--2--0---------  ‚Üê Start on F chord
A|-----------4s3----  ‚Üê Land on C chord (with slide!)
E|------------------

Count:  1   2   3   4  (1 = arrive at C)
              ‚Üë
            Slide down from 4th to 3rd fret!
```

**Step-by-step:**
1. **Beat 1:** D string, 3rd fret (root of F)
2. **Beat 2:** D string, 2nd fret
3. **Beat 3:** D string, open (0)
4. **Beat 4:** A string, 4th fret ‚Üí **slide** to 3rd fret (C chord root!)

**The slide adds bluesy character!**

**Slide technique:**
- Fret the 4th fret with ring finger
- Pluck the string
- Immediately slide finger down to 3rd fret (don't lift off!)
- The note "scoops" down

```
D|------------------
A|--3--------0--2---  
E|--------3---------  ‚Üê Movement on bass while staying on C!

Finger: Ring   Ring  Index  Middle
Count:  1      2     3      4      (back to 1 = C chord again)
```

**The notes:** C (A, 3rd) ‚Üí G (E, 3rd) ‚Üí A (A, open) ‚Üí B (A, 2nd) ‚Üí **C** (A, 3rd)

**What's happening:**
- We start on C chord root (A string, 3rd fret)
- Drop to G note (E string, 3rd fret - the 5th of C chord)
- Walk back UP chromatically to C
- Never left the C chord, but created movement!

**This fills up time during long C chord sections!**

## üé∏ COMMON PROBLEMS & SOLUTIONS

### Problem #1: Bass Notes Landing Off-Beat

**Cause:** Trying to play too fast, not using metronome

**Solutions:**
- Drop tempo by 10 BPM
- Use metronome religiously
- Count out loud: "1-2-3-4"
- Practice bass notes ONLY (no strumming) until timing perfect
- Record yourself - you'll hear timing errors clearly

---

### Problem #2: Left Hand Can't Shift Fast Enough

**Cause:** Hand position not prepared, fingers too tense

**Solutions:**
- Practice walk in SLOW MOTION (10 BPM!)
- Pre-position fingers for next chord while walking
- Keep thumb behind neck (not gripping)
- Relax hand completely between walks
- Practice JUST the hand position changes (no playing)

---

### Problem #3: Walk Sounds Choppy, Not Smooth

**Cause:** Letting notes ring too long OR cutting them off too early

**Solutions:**
- Each bass note should ring until next note plays
- Don't mute strings between notes
- Use metronome - each note gets exactly one beat
- Think "connect" not "separate"
- Listen to country records - hear how smooth walks flow

---

### Problem #4: Can't Hear Bass Notes Over Strumming

**Cause:** Strumming too loud, bass notes too quiet

**Solutions:**
- Pluck bass notes HARDER (with authority!)
- Strum more gently during walks
- Bass notes are the FOUNDATION - they should be heard!
- Practice bass-only for a few days
- Consider using thumb for bass notes (louder than fingers)

---

### Problem #5: Forgetting Which Walk Goes Where

**Cause:** Learning all walks at once, not enough repetition

**Solutions:**
- Learn ONE walk per day (don't rush!)
- Practice each walk 50+ times before moving to next
- Write out the progression: C‚ÜíF (walk #1), F‚ÜíC (walk #2), etc.
- Use only 2 walks for first week (C‚ÜíF, F‚ÜíC)
- Add walks #3 & #4 in week 2

---

### Problem #6: Blues Walks Sound Same as Country Walks

**Cause:** Not emphasizing the chromatic passing tones

**Solutions:**
- Play chromatic notes LOUDER (they're the "flavor"!)
- Practice blues walks at 15 BPM (super slow to hear each note)
- Listen to blues records - hear the chromatic sound
- Sing the note names while playing (hear the half-steps)
- Compare: Play country walk, then blues walk - hear difference

---

### Problem #7: Slide in Blues Walk Sounds Sloppy

**Cause:** Lifting finger during slide, or sliding too slowly

**Solutions:**
- Maintain pressure throughout entire slide
- Slide should be QUICK (like a grace note)
- Practice slide motion without plucking (just feel)
- Use more finger pressure (but not death grip!)
- Start slide closer to target fret if struggling

- Coordination of picking and fretting

- Voice leading (bass line as melody)
