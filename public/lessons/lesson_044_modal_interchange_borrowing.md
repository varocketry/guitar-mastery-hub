## üìñ SECTION 1: WHAT IS MODAL INTERCHANGE?

### The Basic Concept

**Modal interchange (also called "modal mixture" or "borrowed chords"):**

**Temporarily borrowing chords from the parallel major or minor key.**

**Parallel keys = same tonic, different key signatures**

**Examples:**
- **C major** and **C minor** are parallel
- **A minor** and **A major** are parallel
- **E major** and **E minor** are parallel

**Same starting note (tonic), different scale degrees.**

---

### Why It Works

**Because parallel keys share the same tonic:**
- The "home" note is the same
- Borrowed chords still feel connected to the key
- Creates surprise without sounding "wrong"
- Adds emotional color

**Think of it like:**
- Major key = sunny day
- Borrowed minor chords = sudden clouds passing through
- Still the same place, but different mood

---

### Modal Interchange vs. Modulation

**Modal interchange (borrowing):**
- Temporarily use chord(s) from parallel key
- Key center stays the same
- Creates color, not key change
- **Feels like: "Interesting chord choice!"**

**Modulation:**
- Actually change to a different key
- New key center established
- Stays in new key for extended time
- **Feels like: "We moved to a new key"**

**Today we focus on borrowing, not modulating.**

## üìñ SECTION 2: BORROWING FROM PARALLEL MINOR (IN MAJOR KEYS)

### The Setup

**You're in a MAJOR key (bright, happy).**

**You borrow chords from the PARALLEL MINOR (dark, sad).**

**Result: Temporary darkening, emotional depth.**

---

### Common Borrowed Chords in Major Keys

**In C Major, you can borrow from C Minor:**

**C Major diatonic chords:**  
C - Dm - Em - F - G - Am - Bdim

**C Minor diatonic chords:**  
Cm - Ddim - Eb - Fm - Gm - Ab - Bb

**Most common borrowed chords:**

1. **iv (minor subdominant)** - Instead of IV (major)
   - C major: F ‚Üí **Fm** (borrowed from C minor)
   - Creates: Melancholic, bittersweet quality

2. **‚ô≠VII (subtonic major)** - From natural minor
   - C major: Add **Bb** (borrowed from C minor)
   - Creates: Rock sound, modal flavor

3. **‚ô≠VI (submediant major)** - From natural minor
   - C major: Add **Ab** (borrowed from C minor)
   - Creates: Dramatic, descending effect

4. **‚ô≠III (mediant major)** - From natural minor
   - C major: Add **Eb** (borrowed from C minor)
   - Creates: Unexpected, dramatic

5. **ii¬∞ (diminished supertonic)** - From natural minor
   - C major: Dm ‚Üí **Ddim** (borrowed from C minor)
   - Creates: Tension, leading quality

---

### The Most Important: iv (Minor Subdominant)

**Borrowing iv from parallel minor is THE most common borrowed chord.**

**In C Major:**
- Diatonic: F (IV) = F-A-C (major)
- Borrowed: **Fm (iv)** = F-Ab-C (minor) ‚Üê from C minor

**Example progression:**

**C - Fm - C - G**

Instead of: C - F - C - G (all diatonic)

**The Fm adds darkness and emotional depth.**

---

### Playing iv (Minor Subdominant) Borrowed Chord

**C Major progression with borrowed iv:**

**C - Fm - G - C**

```
C:            Fm:           G:            C:
E|---0---|    E|---1---|    E|---3---|    E|---0---|
B|---1---|    B|---1---|    B|---0---|    B|---1---|
G|---0---|    G|---1---|    G|---0---|    G|---0---|
D|---2---|    D|---3---|    D|---0---|    D|---2---|
A|---3---|    A|---3---|    A|---2---|    A|---3---|
E|-------|    E|---1---|    E|---3---|    E|-------|
```

**Listen for:**
- C major sounds bright
- **Fm suddenly darkens** the progression
- G brings it back
- C resolves, but with added depth

**This is modal interchange in action.**

---

### Famous Example: "Creep" - Radiohead

**Key: G major**

**Progression: G - B - C - Cm**

**Analysis:**
- G = I (major tonic)
- B = III (major mediant - already unusual!)
- C = IV (major subdominant)
- **Cm = iv (borrowed from G minor!)** ‚Üê Modal interchange!

**The Cm chord creates the haunting, dark quality of the song.**

**Without that borrowed iv, "Creep" wouldn't sound like "Creep."**

## üìñ SECTION 3: BORROWING FROM PARALLEL MAJOR (IN MINOR KEYS)

### The Setup

**You're in a MINOR key (dark, sad).**

**You borrow chords from the PARALLEL MAJOR (bright, happy).**

**Result: Temporary brightening, hopeful moment.**

---

### Common Borrowed Chords in Minor Keys

**In A Minor, you can borrow from A Major:**

**A Minor diatonic chords:**  
Am - Bdim - C - Dm - Em - F - G

**A Major diatonic chords:**  
A - Bm - C#m - D - E - F#m - G#dim

**Most common borrowed chords:**

1. **IV (major subdominant)** - Instead of iv (minor)
   - A minor: Dm ‚Üí **D** (borrowed from A major)
   - Creates: Brief brightening, hopeful quality

2. **II (major supertonic)** - Instead of ii¬∞ (diminished)
   - A minor: Bdim ‚Üí **B** (borrowed from A major)
   - Creates: Strong pre-dominant, classical sound

3. **VII (major leading tone)** - Instead of ‚ô≠VII
   - A minor: G ‚Üí **G#** (borrowed from A major)
   - Creates: Strong leading tone pull
   - Note: This is similar to using harmonic minor V

---

### Borrowing IV (Major Subdominant)

**In A Minor:**
- Diatonic: Dm (iv) = D-F-A (minor)
- Borrowed: **D (IV)** = D-F#-A (major) ‚Üê from A major

**Example progression:**

**Am - D - G - Am**

Instead of: Am - Dm - G - Am (all diatonic minor)

**The D major adds brightness and hope to the minor key.**

---

### Playing IV (Major Subdominant) Borrowed Chord

**A Minor progression with borrowed IV:**

**Am - D - E - Am**

```
Am:           D:            E:            Am:
E|---0---|    E|---2---|    E|---0---|    E|---0---|
B|---1---|    B|---3---|    B|---0---|    B|---1---|
G|---2---|    G|---2---|    G|---1---|    G|---2---|
D|---2---|    D|---0---|    D|---2---|    D|---2---|
A|---0---|    A|-------|    A|---2---|    A|---0---|
E|-------|    E|-------|    E|---0---|    E|-------|
```

**Listen for:**
- Am sounds dark
- **D major brightens** briefly
- E creates tension
- Am resolves with added complexity

## üìñ SECTION 4: THE MOST USEFUL BORROWED CHORDS

### In Major Keys (Borrowing from Parallel Minor)

**Priority 1: iv (minor subdominant)**
- Example: C major borrowing Fm
- Effect: Bittersweet, melancholic
- Used constantly in pop, rock, film scores

**Priority 2: ‚ô≠VII (subtonic major)**
- Example: C major borrowing Bb
- Effect: Rock/modal sound
- Used in rock, blues-rock, modal music

**Priority 3: ‚ô≠VI (submediant major)**
- Example: C major borrowing Ab
- Effect: Dramatic descent
- Used in dramatic moments, film scores

---

### In Minor Keys (Borrowing from Parallel Major)

**Priority 1: IV (major subdominant)**
- Example: A minor borrowing D
- Effect: Brief brightness, hope
- Used in minor key songs needing lift

**Priority 2: II (major supertonic)**
- Example: A minor borrowing B
- Effect: Strong pre-dominant function
- Used in classical-influenced music

## üìñ SECTION 6: PRACTICAL APPLICATIONS

### Application 1: Adding Emotional Depth

**Take a simple major progression:**

**C - Am - F - G** (I-vi-IV-V in C major)

**Add borrowed iv:**

**C - Am - Fm - G** (Fm borrowed from C minor)

**Result:** More emotional, bittersweet quality

---

### Application 2: Creating Surprise

**Take a predictable progression:**

**C - F - G - C** (I-IV-V-I in C major)

**Add borrowed ‚ô≠VII:**

**C - F - Bb - C** (Bb borrowed from C minor)

**Result:** Unexpected, rock-influenced sound

---

### Application 3: Brightening Minor Keys

**Take a dark minor progression:**

**Am - Dm - Em - Am** (i-iv-v-i in A minor)

**Add borrowed IV:**

**Am - D - E - Am** (D major borrowed from A major)

**Result:** Brief hope, brightness in darkness
