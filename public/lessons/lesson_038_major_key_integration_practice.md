### The Five Core Skills of Major Key Mastery

**Skill 1: Scale Construction**
- Given any root note, build the major scale using W-W-H-W-W-W-H
- Example: "Build Eb major scale" ‚Üí Eb-F-G-Ab-Bb-C-D-Eb

**Skill 2: Key Signature Recognition**
- Given sharps or flats, identify the key instantly
- Example: See 4 sharps ‚Üí Know it's E major

**Skill 3: Diatonic Chord Construction**
- Given a key, build all seven diatonic chords
- Example: "What are the diatonic chords in A major?" ‚Üí A-Bm-C#m-D-E-F#m-G#dim

**Skill 4: Harmonic Analysis**
- Given a chord progression, identify Roman numerals and function
- Example: G-C-D-G ‚Üí I-IV-V-I (Tonic-Subdominant-Dominant-Tonic)

**Skill 5: Voice Leading Application**
- Given a progression, add inversions for smooth voice leading
- Example: C-F-G-C ‚Üí C-F/A-G/B-C (smoother bass line)

**Master these five skills, and you've mastered major key theory.**

## üìñ SECTION 2: COMPREHENSIVE INTEGRATION EXERCISES

### Integration Exercise 1: Key of G Major (Complete Analysis)

**Task: Demonstrate complete understanding of G major**

---

**Step 1: Build the G Major Scale**

Formula: W-W-H-W-W-W-H

```
G ‚Üí A = 2 frets (W)
A ‚Üí B = 2 frets (W)
B ‚Üí C = 1 fret (H)
C ‚Üí D = 2 frets (W)
D ‚Üí E = 2 frets (W)
E ‚Üí F# = 2 frets (W)
F# ‚Üí G = 1 fret (H)

G Major Scale: G - A - B - C - D - E - F# - G
```

‚úú Scale degree names: Tonic (G), Supertonic (A), Mediant (B), Subdominant (C), Dominant (D), Submediant (E), Leading Tone (F#)

---

**Step 2: Identify the Key Signature**

G major has **1 sharp: F#**

Why? Because the major scale formula requires F# (not F) between E and G for the correct whole step.

**Circle of Fifths position:** One step clockwise from C major

---

**Step 3: Build All Diatonic Chords**

Pattern: I-ii-iii-IV-V-vi-vii√Ç¬∞

```
I   = G major (G-B-D)
ii  = A minor (A-C-E)
iii = B minor (B-D-F#)
IV  = C major (C-E-G)
V   = D major (D-F#-A)
vi  = E minor (E-G-B)
vii√Ç¬∞= F# diminished (F#-A-C)
```

---

**Step 4: Analyze Common Progressions in G Major**

**Progression 1: G - C - D - G**
- Roman Numerals: I - IV - V - I
- Function: Tonic ‚Üí Subdominant ‚Üí Dominant ‚Üí Tonic
- Character: Strong, complete, resolved
- Used in: Thousands of folk and country songs

**Progression 2: G - D - Em - C**
- Roman Numerals: I - V - vi - IV
- Function: Tonic ‚Üí Dominant ‚Üí Relative Minor ‚Üí Subdominant
- Character: Pop, emotional, very singable
- Used in: "Let It Be" pattern in G major

**Progression 3: G - Em - C - D**
- Roman Numerals: I - vi - IV - V
- Function: Classic '50s progression
- Character: Smooth, nostalgic
- Used in: "Stand By Me" pattern in G major

---

**Step 5: Add Voice Leading with Inversions**

**Original: G - C - D - G**  
Bass jumps: G ‚Üí C ‚Üí D ‚Üí G

**With inversions: G - C/E - D/F# - G**  
Bass ascends smoothly: G ‚Üí E ‚Üí F# ‚Üí G

**Or: G - C - D/F# - G**  
Bass: G ‚Üí C ‚Üí F# ‚Üí G (smoother return to G)

---

**‚úú Complete G Major Integration Demonstrated**

## üìñ SECTION 3: SONG ANALYSIS PRACTICE

### Analyzing "Stand By Me" (Ben E. King) - Key of A Major

**Chord progression: A - F#m - D - E**

---

**Step 1: Identify the Key**

Song centers on A, chords contain C# and F# (key signature of A major = 3 sharps)

**Key: A Major**

---

**Step 2: Build A Major Scale**

**A - B - C# - D - E - F# - G# - A**

---

**Step 3: Identify Diatonic Chords**

```
I   = A major
ii  = B minor
iii = C# minor
IV  = D major
V   = E major
vi  = F# minor
vii√Ç¬∞= G# diminished
```

---

**Step 4: Analyze the Progression**

**A - F#m - D - E**

- A = I (Tonic)
- F#m = vi (Submediant, relative minor)
- D = IV (Subdominant)
- E = V (Dominant)

**Roman Numerals: I - vi - IV - V**

**Function:**
- Starts at home (I)
- Moves to relative minor (vi) for emotional color
- Leaves home via subdominant (IV)
- Creates tension with dominant (V)
- (Loops back to I for resolution)

**This is the classic '50s progression.**

---

**Step 5: Voice Leading Analysis**

**Bass line (root position): A - F# - D - E**

Large jump from A to F#, then another jump to D.

**With inversions: A - F#m/A - D - E**

Bass: A - A - D - E (smoother, keeps A longer)

**Or: A - F#m - D/F# - E**

Bass: A - F# - F# - E (holds F#, then smooth to E)

### Preview: Relative Minor

**Every major key has a relative minor** that shares the same key signature.

**Examples:**
- C major / A minor (both have no sharps/flats)
- G major / E minor (both have 1 sharp: F#)
- D major / B minor (both have 2 sharps: F#, C#)

**Relative minor = 6th scale degree of major scale**

In C major: C-D-E-F-G-**A**-B-C  
A is the 6th ‚Üí A minor is relative minor

**This relationship will be crucial in Theory Module 2.**
