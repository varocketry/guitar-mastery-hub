### The Three Revelations About Lead Guitar

**Revelation 1: Lead Guitar is Simpler Than Rhythm**

**Eric Clapton:** *"People think lead guitar is advanced, rhythm is basic. That's backwards. A barre chord progression at tempo with perfect dynamics is harder than most blues solos. Lead guitar is actually simpler technically - you're playing one note at a time instead of 4-6 simultaneously. But it FEELS exposed because there's nowhere to hide."*

**You're already technically capable.** If you can hold a barre chord and switch smoothly, you can play lead guitar. The fingers are stronger, the dexterity is there, the coordination exists.

**What's new is the mindset:** Single notes instead of chords. Melody instead of harmony.

**Revelation 2: Lead Guitar Uses Shapes (Just Like Chords)**

Remember Lesson 4? Den Lopez's revolutionary insight: "Learn shapes, not isolated fingerings."

**Same principle for lead guitar.**

**B.B. King:** *"Everyone asks how I know where to play. I tell them: I learned five shapes. Just five. That's the pentatonic scale in five positions. Every solo I've ever played - and I mean EVERY solo across 70 years - comes from those five shapes. People think I'm a genius improviser. I'm just moving shapes around like you move chord shapes. **The pattern you're learning today:**
- 5 notes (pentatonic scale)
- 1 position (box pattern)  
- Movable anywhere (just like chords)

**This one pattern unlocks:**

### Why Single Notes Feel Different

**When you play a chord:**
- Multiple strings provide safety net
- Small mistakes get masked
- Full, big sound
- Hiding space

**When you play single notes:**
- Every note is exposed
- Every mistake is obvious
- Clear, precise sound  
- No hiding

**David Gilmour** (Pink Floyd): *"Lead guitar terrifies beginners because there's nowhere to hide. You play one wrong note in a chord? Might not even hear it. You play one wrong note in a solo? **This is why starting with rhythm guitar first was smart:**

- Now you're ready for the exposure of single notes

### The Malcolm Young Method Applied to Lead

**"Slow is Smooth, Smooth is Fast"**

This principle is EVEN MORE CRITICAL for lead guitar.

**Why?**
- Single notes = every imperfection is audible
- Speed without accuracy = noise, not music
- Muscle memory forms from whatever you practice
- Bad habits are harder to fix in lead playing

**Starting tempo for lead guitar: 40 BPM**

**Joe Satriani:** *"I teach alternate picking starting at 40 BPM. Students think I'm crazy. 'That's way too slow,' they say. I tell them: 'If it's too slow, you can't play it wrong. And if you can't play it wrong, you'll learn it right.' Two weeks at 40 BPM creates permanent good habits. Two weeks at 100 BPM creates permanent struggles."*

**The progression:**
- Week 1: 40 BPM (boring but building perfection)
- Week 2: 50 BPM (starting to feel musical)
- Week 3: 60 BPM (sounds like actual guitar playing)
- Week 4: 70 BPM (approaching musical tempo)
- Month 2: 80-100 BPM (most songs live here)
- Month 3+: 100-140 BPM (fast lead guitar)

**What "Pentatonic" Means:**
- **Penta** = Five
- **Tonic** = Notes/Tones
- **Pentatonic** = Five-note scale

**E Minor Pentatonic Notes:**
- E (root note)

**Why these 5 notes?**

## √∞≈∏‚Äù¬ß TROUBLESHOOTING GUIDE

### Problem 1: Alternate Picking Feels Awkward

**Symptoms:**
- Up-strokes feel weak or uncertain
- Defaulting to all down-strokes
- Inconsistent rhythm between down/up

**Solutions:**

1. **Slow Down More**
   - Try 30 BPM if 40 feels rushed
   - Perfect technique before speed

2. **Isolate Up-Strokes**
   - Practice ONLY up-strokes for 2 minutes
   - Build confidence in motion

3. **Check Pick Grip**
   - Loose enough to move freely
   - Firm enough to not drop
   - Wrist motion (not arm motion)

---

### Problem 2: Notes Buzzing or Muted

**Symptoms:**
- Some notes don't ring clearly
- Buzzing sound on certain frets
- Inconsistent tone

**Solutions:**

1. **Press Closer to Fret**
   - Finger should be right next to fret wire
   - Not in middle of fret space

2. **More Finger Pressure**
   - Single notes require firm press
   - Build finger strength gradually

3. **Check Finger Angle**
   - Should be perpendicular to fretboard
   - On fingertip, not pad

---

### Problem 3: Can't Remember Pattern

**Symptoms:**
- Forgetting which notes come next
- Getting lost in scale
- Breaking rhythm to think

**Solutions:**

1. **Visual Diagram**
   - Print the fretboard diagram
   - Keep visible while practicing
   - Reference until memorized

2. **String-by-String**
   - Only practice one string per day
   - Master before adding next
   - Don't rush the process

3. **Say Note Names**
   - While playing: "E, G, A, B..."
   - Auditory + muscle memory
   - Faster memorization
