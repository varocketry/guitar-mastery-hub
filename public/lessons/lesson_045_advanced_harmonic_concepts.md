**What if learning ONE sophisticated chord could empower you to write five songs?**

Paul McCartney was a teenager when he walked into Hessy's music shop in Liverpool with George Harrison. Behind the counter was Jim Gretty, a jazz guitarist who worked as a salesman. When Gretty played an F7#9 chord, Paul and George stopped cold. "Wow, what was that, man?" they asked. Gretty showed them: "It's just basically an F, but you barre the top two strings at the fourth fret with your little finger."

That moment changed everything. Paul recalls: "You know, that F7#9 is a **beautiful chord**, and it was **outside our frame of reference** because nothing that we listened to contained that chord. But here's the profound insight Paul shares: "Me and John would come in with our acoustics and start with E, A and B chords, and then we'd throw in, like, a C# minor seventh. **It'd be very exciting ‚Äì I still remember the palpable thrill of finding a chord that we hadn't used before. It can empower you to write five songs**, or help make the one you're writing better."

Think about that. The most successful songwriting team in history got **excited** about learning new chords. They didn't feel intimidated by extended harmonies - they felt **empowered**. Each sophisticated chord they discovered opened new creative possibilities, unlocking melodies and progressions they couldn't have imagined with just basic major and minor chords.

He discusses how "for me, it's all about chords" - showing how C minor 7ths suggested "Penny Lane," how discovering new chord voicings led to Beatles innovations, how paying attention to harmonics hidden in chords created musical magic.

## üìñ SECTION 1: SECONDARY DOMINANTS

### What Is a Secondary Dominant?

**Secondary dominant:** A dominant chord that resolves to a chord OTHER than the tonic.

**In simple terms:** Treating a non-tonic chord as if it were temporarily the tonic.

**Remember:**
- In C major, V (G major) resolves to I (C major)
- That's the primary dominant

**Secondary dominants:**
- Treat other chords as temporary tonics
- Create V-I relationships to those chords
- Add color and forward motion

---

### The Most Common: V/V (Five of Five)

**"V/V" = The dominant of the dominant**

**In C Major:**
- V = G major
- What's the dominant of G major? D major
- So V/V = D major

**Progression example:**

**C - D - G - C**

Instead of: C - Dm - G - C (diatonic)

**Analysis:**
- C = I (tonic)
- **D major = V/V** (dominant of G, not in C major diatonically!)
- G = V (dominant, feels like temporary tonic)
- C = I (actual tonic, resolution)

**The D major chord creates temporary focus on G, then G resolves to C.**

---

### Playing V/V on Guitar

**C Major with V/V:**

**C - D - G - C**

```
C:            D:            G:            C:
E|---0---|    E|---2---|    E|---3---|    E|---0---|
B|---1---|    B|---3---|    B|---0---|    B|---1---|
G|---0---|    G|---2---|    G|---0---|    G|---0---|
D|---2---|    D|---0---|    D|---0---|    D|---2---|
A|---3---|    A|-------|    A|---2---|    A|---3---|
E|-------|    E|-------|    E|---3---|    E|-------|
```

**Listen for:**
- C sounds like home
- **D major creates expectation** (not diatonic to C major!)
- G feels like a resolution (temporary tonic)
- C is the final resolution

**This is V/V in action.**

---

### Other Common Secondary Dominants

**V/ii (Dominant of ii):**

In C major: A major ‚Üí Dm

**Progression: C - A - Dm - G - C**
- A major = V/ii (dominant of Dm)
- Creates temporary focus on Dm

---

**V/vi (Dominant of vi):**

In C major: E major ‚Üí Am

**Progression: C - E - Am - F - G - C**
- E major = V/vi (dominant of Am)
- Creates temporary focus on Am

---

**V/IV (Dominant of IV):**

In C major: C major ‚Üí F

**Progression: C - C7 - F - G - C**
- C7 (C major with ‚ô≠7) = V/IV (dominant of F)
- Creates temporary focus on F

---

### Why Secondary Dominants Work

**They create temporary key centers:**
- Music doesn't actually modulate (change keys)
- But briefly FEELS like another key
- Adds forward motion and interest
- Creates expectation and resolution

**Used extensively in:**
- Classical music (Bach, Mozart)
- Jazz standards
- Pop music (sophisticated progressions)
- Film scores

## üìñ SECTION 2: DIMINISHED SEVENTH CHORDS

### What Is a Diminished Seventh Chord?

**Diminished seventh chord (dim7 or √Ç¬∞7):**
- Built from stacked minor thirds
- Four notes, all equally spaced
- Extremely tense, unstable sound
- Resolves to nearby chords

---

### Building Diminished Seventh Chords

**Structure:** Root - ‚ô≠3 - ‚ô≠5 - ‚ô≠‚ô≠7 (double flat 7 = 6th)

**B Diminished Seventh (B√Ç¬∞7):**
```
B  - D  - F  - Ab
1    ‚ô≠3   ‚ô≠5   ‚ô≠‚ô≠7
```

**Equally spaced minor thirds (3 half steps each):**
```
B ‚Üí D   = 3 half steps (minor 3rd)
D ‚Üí F   = 3 half steps (minor 3rd)
F ‚Üí Ab  = 3 half steps (minor 3rd)
Ab ‚Üí B  = 3 half steps (minor 3rd) - back to start!
```

**This symmetry is unique.**

---

### The Magic of Diminished Seventh Chords

**Because of the symmetry:**
- B√Ç¬∞7 = D√Ç¬∞7 = F√Ç¬∞7 = Ab√Ç¬∞7 (all the same notes!)
- Only 3 unique dim7 chords exist (12 notes √É¬∑ 4 = 3)
- Each has 4 names
- Can resolve to multiple places

**This makes dim7 extremely versatile.**

---

### Common Uses of Diminished Seventh

**Use 1: Passing Chord**

Move between two nearby chords smoothly.

**Example in C major:**

**C - C#dim7 - Dm - G - C**
- C = I
- C#dim7 = passing chord (between C and Dm)
- Dm = ii
- Creates smooth chromatic movement

---

**Use 2: Leading Tone Diminished Seventh**

**vii√Ç¬∞7 in any key** (built on 7th scale degree)

**In C major: B√Ç¬∞7 ‚Üí C**
- B√Ç¬∞7 = B-D-F-Ab
- Resolves strongly to C major
- Creates intense pull to tonic

---

**Use 3: Substitute for V7**

**Diminished seventh can substitute for dominant seventh:**

Instead of G7 ‚Üí C, use B√Ç¬∞7 ‚Üí C

**Both create strong resolution to C.**

---

### Playing Diminished Seventh Chords

**B√Ç¬∞7 (Leading tone in C major):**

```
E|---2---|
B|---3---|
G|---1---|
D|---2---|
A|---2---|
E|-------|
```

**Or (different voicing):**

```

G|---4---|
D|---3---|

```

**Use in progression:**

**C - B√Ç¬∞7 - C**

Creates tension and resolution.

## üìñ SECTION 3: AUGMENTED SIXTH CHORDS

### What Are Augmented Sixth Chords?

**Augmented sixth chords:** Pre-dominant chords containing an augmented sixth interval.

**They strongly pull to the dominant (V).**

**Three types:**
1. **Italian Sixth (It+6)**
2. **French Sixth (Fr+6)**
3. **German Sixth (Gr+6)**

**All resolve to V (dominant).**

---

### The Italian Sixth (It+6)

**In C major, resolving to G (V):**

**Structure: ‚ô≠6 - 1 - #4**

In C major: Ab - C - F#

**Creates augmented sixth interval:**
- Ab to F# = augmented sixth (9 half steps)
- Resolves outward: Ab‚ÜíG, F#‚ÜíG
- Both notes resolve to G (the dominant)

**Sound:** Dramatic, classical, intense

---

### The French Sixth (Fr+6)

**Adds a major third above the bass:**

**Structure: ‚ô≠6 - 1 - 2 - #4**

In C major: Ab - C - D - F#

**Still has augmented sixth (Ab-F#), plus added color (D).**

---

### The German Sixth (Gr+6)

**Adds a perfect fifth above the bass:**

**Structure: ‚ô≠6 - 1 - ‚ô≠3 - #4**

In C major: Ab - C - Eb - F#

**Most common of the three.**

**Often spelled enharmonically to avoid augmented sixth:**
- Ab - C - Eb - F# (German sixth)
- Or: Ab - C - Eb - Gb (looks like Ab7)

**Functions identically.**

---

### When to Use Augmented Sixth Chords

**Use before the dominant (V):**

**C - Gr+6 - G - C**

Creates dramatic approach to G, then resolution to C.

**Used in:**
- Classical music (Romantic era)
- Film scores (dramatic moments)
- Jazz (sophisticated pre-dominants)
- Prog rock

**Not common in simple pop, but adds sophistication.**

## üìñ SECTION 4: THE NEAPOLITAN SIXTH CHORD

### What Is the Neapolitan Sixth?

**Neapolitan sixth (N6 or ‚ô≠II6):**
- A major chord built on the lowered second scale degree
- Used in first inversion (6th in bass)
- Pre-dominant function (moves to V or i)
- Exotic, dramatic sound

---

### Building the Neapolitan Sixth

**In C minor:**

**C minor scale:** C - D - Eb - F - G - Ab - Bb - C

**‚ô≠II = Db (lowered 2nd degree)**

**Db major chord: Db - F - Ab**

**In first inversion (6th in bass): F - Ab - Db**
- F in bass (6th of Db)
- Symbol: N6 or ‚ô≠II6

---

### Neapolitan Sixth in Progressions

**Common in minor keys:**

**i - N6 - V - i**

**In A minor:**

**Am - Bb/D - E - Am**
- Am = i (tonic)
- Bb in first inversion = N6 (F in bass)
- E = V (dominant)
- Am = i (resolution)

**Creates exotic, dramatic color before the dominant.**

---

### Playing Neapolitan Sixth

**In A Minor:**

**Am - Bb/D - E - Am**

```
Am:           Bb/D:         E:            Am:
E|---0---|    E|---1---|    E|---0---|    E|---0---|
B|---1---|    B|---3---|    B|---0---|    B|---1---|
G|---2---|    G|---3---|    G|---1---|    G|---2---|
D|---2---|    D|---3---|    D|---2---|    D|---2---|
A|---0---|    A|---0---|    A|---2---|    A|---0---|
E|-------|    E|-------|    E|---0---|    E|-------|
```

**Listen for the exotic, dramatic quality before the dominant.**

---

### Why It's Called "Neapolitan"

**Associated with the Neapolitan school of opera (Italy, 1700s).**

Composers like Alessandro Scarlatti used it extensively.

**Now it's a standard classical harmony tool.**

## üìñ SECTION 5: ADVANCED VOICE LEADING

### Chromatic Voice Leading

**Moving voices by half steps (chromatically) for smooth connections.**

**Example:**

**C - C#dim7 - Dm**

```
Voice 1: C ‚Üí C# ‚Üí D  (chromatic ascent)
Voice 2: E ‚Üí E  ‚Üí F  (half step up)
Voice 3: G ‚Üí G  ‚Üí A  (whole step up)
```

**Each voice moves smoothly, creating seamless transition.**

---

### Voice Leading with Secondary Dominants

**When using V/V (D major in C major):**

**C - D - G - C**

**Good voice leading:**
```
C chord:  C - E - G
D chord:  D - F# - A  (E‚ÜíF# is half step)
G chord:  G - B - D   (F#‚ÜíG, A‚ÜíB stepwise)
C chord:  C - E - G   (smooth return)
```

**Minimize jumps, maximize stepwise motion.**

---

### Avoiding Parallel Fifths and Octaves

**Classical rule (still relevant):**

Don't move two voices in parallel perfect fifths or octaves.

**Why?** Creates hollow, less rich sound.

**Example of what NOT to do:**

```
Voice 1: C ‚Üí G (perfect fifth)
Voice 2: G ‚Üí D (perfect fifth)
Both move up a perfect fifth = parallel fifths
```

**Solution:** Move one voice differently to break parallelism.

## üìñ SECTION 6: PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER

### Sophisticated Progression Example

**Using multiple advanced concepts:**

**C - A - Dm - Gr+6 - G - C**

**Analysis:**
- C = I (tonic)
- A = V/ii (secondary dominant to Dm)
- Dm = ii (temporary tonic, then subdominant)
- Gr+6 = German sixth (dramatic approach to G)
- G = V (dominant)
- C = I (resolution)

**This progression uses:**
- Secondary dominant (A major)
- Advanced pre-dominant (German sixth)
- Smooth voice leading throughout
- **Graduate-level sophistication**

---

### When to Use Advanced Concepts

**Use secondary dominants when:**
- You want forward motion
- You want to emphasize a non-tonic chord
- You want sophisticated color
- You're writing jazz or classical-influenced music

**Use diminished seventh when:**
- You need a passing chord
- You want tension
- You're creating chromatic voice leading
- You want versatility (can resolve many ways)

**Use augmented sixth/Neapolitan when:**
- You want dramatic approach to dominant
- You're writing classical or film score-inspired music
- You want exotic, sophisticated color
- You want to sound like a master composer

**Don't use when:**
- Simple diatonic sounds better for the song
- Genre doesn't call for sophistication (punk, basic pop)
- It distracts from melody/lyrics

**Advanced harmony is a tool, not a requirement.**
